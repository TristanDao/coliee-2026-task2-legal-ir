In coming to this conclusion, the 29 May panel examined the power of a panel of the Convention and Refugee Determination Division to re-open a decision reached by another panel. The panel noted that it is settled law that an administrative tribunal created by statute, in the absence of express statutory authority, has no general power to set aside its own decisions. (The panel referred to the following decisions:
Lugano v. Minister of Manpower and Immigration
, [1977] 2 F.C. 605; 15 N.R. 254 (C.A.);
Longia v. Minister of Employment and Immigration
, [1990] 3 F.C. 288; 114 N.R. 280 (C.A.) (hereinafter
Longia
);
Agbona v. Canada (Minister of Employment and Immigration)
(1993), 66 F.T.R. 191; 21 Imm. L.R.(2d) 279 (F.C.T.D.) per Noël, J.;
Camacho-Souza v. Minister of Employment and Immigration
(1994), 74 F.T.R. 208 (T.D.) per Wetston, J.; and
Chaudhry v. Canada (Minister of Employment and Immigration)
(1994), 83 F.T.R. 81; 25 Imm. L.R.(2d) 139 (F.C.T.D.) per Nadon, J. (hereinafter
Chaudhry
].) The panel noted, however, that a decision may be revisited in common law where the issue which was referred to the decision-maker has not been dealt with. (
Severud v. Canada Employment and Immigration Commission
, [1991] 2 F.C. 318; 127 N.R. 152 (F.C.A.).) According to the tribunal, Macaulay, in
Practice and Procedure before Administrative Tribunals
, notes that a decision may be re-opened where the error has affected the integrity of the decision-making process. ((Toronto: Carswell, 1994) at 27A-41.) The CRDD may reopen a decision where there has been a denial of natural justice at the original hearing. This was established in
Woldu v. Minister of Manpower and Immigration
, [1978] 2 F.C. 216; 18 N.R. 46 (F.C.A.) (hereinafter
Woldu
) as quoted in
Gill v. Minister of Employment and Immigration
, [1987] 2 F.C. 425; 80 N.R. 1 (F.C.A.) (hereinafter
Gill
), where the court held:
"Notwithstanding the general principle, affirmed in the
Lugano
case, that an administrative tribunal does not have the power, in the absence of express statutory authority, to set aside its decision, there is judicial opinion to suggest that where a tribunal recognizes that it has failed to observe the rules of natural justice it may treat its decision as a nullity and rehear the case." (
Woldu
, p. 219)
The CRDD quoted my decision in
Chaudhry
in support of the proposition that a decision may be re-opened where there has been a breach of natural justice.
