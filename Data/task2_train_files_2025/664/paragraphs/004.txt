The record shows that even though the visa officer considered that the applicant demonstrated sufficient ability in English, the latter was advised that she should bring an interpreter if necessary. She chose to show up to the interview without an interpreter. With respect to the right to counsel, the applicant did not ask for one and there is no statutory obligation to assign counsel to visa seekers or to allow them to bring their own (see
Dehghani v. Minister of Employment and Immigration
, [1993] 1 S.C.R. 1053; 150 N.R. 241). The interview lasted one hour and fifteen minutes, and that, after the claimant had already presented documents to support her request. With respect to confrontation with contradictions in testimony, the visa officer who interviewed the applicant, in his affidavit, states:
"... During the interview, I raised with the applicant my concerns with respect to her fear of persecution upon her return to Iran. I informed the applicant that in my opinion, she had experienced the normal hardship of life in Iran. The applicant did not provide any further information that might have relieved my concerns despite being afforded the opportunity to do so."
