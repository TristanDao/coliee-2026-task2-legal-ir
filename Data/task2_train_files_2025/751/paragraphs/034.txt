The Prothonotary found the Crown had not met its burden of demonstrating that it was plain and obvious that the action could not be maintained. The Prothonotary engaged in an analysis of the case-law supporting her finding that the purpose of the
NOC Regulations
is to protect patent holders and generics, which I will not repeat here. On page 8 of the Order, the learned Prothonotary held:
In my view, the present case does not fall within, nor is it analogous to, any category of cases in which a duty of care has previously been recognized by Canadian courts...At the pleadings stage, then, AstraZeneca is entitled to maintain its action unless the Crown, who has the burden in that regard, can demonstrate that it is plain and obvious that the governing statute, as a whole cannot be said, expressly or by implication to give rise to sufficient proximity or "neighbourhood" between AstraZeneca and the Minister...in light of the particular circumstances, the alleged negligence of the Minister inthe processing of Apotex's regulatory submissions, the failure to warn the parties and judicial consideration of the
Regulations,
the Crown, in my view, fails to meet that burden.
Indeed, when looking at the purpose of the
