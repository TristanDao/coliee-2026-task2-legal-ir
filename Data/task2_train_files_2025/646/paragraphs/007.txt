In both his affidavit and statutory declaration, the immigration officer provided more details regarding his decision. In particular, he noted:
a) RSS, whose abilities and knowledge of technical terms in the tool and dye trade was very limited, nonetheless could be considered a tool and dye maker as defined in the Canadian Classification and Dictionary of Occupations (CCDO). He was awarded units of assessment accordingly. This is explained well in paragraphs 7 and 11 of the affidavit.
b) RSS was unable to answer basic questions posed to him in the English language. His knowledge of written English was poor as well. He has no knowledge of French. He was assessed units accordingly, as noted in the visa officer's affidavit.
c) Even with the assistance which RSS would receive from his relatives in Winnipeg, he would have difficulty in becoming successfully established in Canada. He had never previously travelled outside India, and had little concept of life in Canada. He had not prepared for the interview by trying to investigate employment opportunities in Canada for tool and dye makers. All of this put his adaptability, initiative and resourcefulness into question. He was awarded units of assessment accordingly.
d) RSS's wife was also interviewed. She had no knowledge of English or French and has never worked outside the home.
e) Neither RSS or his wife had any other occupational skills with a positive occupational demand factor which would have enabled them to meet immigration requirements.
f) There were insufficient humanitarian and compassionate grounds to warrant admission on that basis. RSS and his family have always lived in India, and most of his wife's siblings also resided in India. RSS was operating his own business and appeared to be relatively well-established.
