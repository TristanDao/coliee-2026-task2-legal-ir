I am satisfied the allegations relating to damages, as set out in the Statement of Claim, are sufficient for the purpose of pleading. McNair, J., dealt with the nature of the particulars required when pleading damages in
Canadian Olympic Assn. v. National Gym Clothing Ltd.
(1984), 2 C.P.R.(3d) 145 (F.T.D.), at pp. 149 and 150:
"Here the defendants ask for particulars in the following terms:
v. particulars in relation to para. 12 of the Statement of Claim, namely the nature of the alleged damages or losses suffered by the plaintiff.
"Paragraph 12 of the Statement of Claim reads:
12. Further, by reason of the acts of the defendant aforesaid, the defendant has made unlawful profit and the plaintiff has suffered damage and loss.
"The concluding portion of the Statement of Claim sets out in lettered subparagraphs or clauses the various claims for relief, among which are:
(g) Damages for the breach and infringement of the plaintiff's official marks and copyright, and damages with respect to copyright, for conversion, or for an accounting of the defendant's profits whichever shall, upon inquiry, prove to the greater sum;
(h) Punitive damages;
"The plaintiff has pleaded the essential allegation of damage and loss.
Obviously, the damages claimed are at large, being general not special
. The defendant has been put on notice that the plaintiff will ask for punitive damages. Whether it gets them or not is another matter which is of no immediate concern. There is no suggestion in the Statement of Claim of any probability of a claim for special damages. Indeed, all indications are to the contrary.
"The rule is clear that general damage need not be specially pleaded, but special damages must be pleaded in order that the defendant may not be taken by surprise at the trial ..." (Emphasis added)
