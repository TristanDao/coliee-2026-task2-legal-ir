The adjudicator's decision to weigh the complaint form "accordingly" implies that he admitted it with an eye to accurately weigh­ing its relevancy or necessity when it came time to develop his reasons. As the master of his own procedure, the adjudicator had the discretion to do this. The admission of the form per se, therefore,
cannot ground the claim that the applicant seeks to make; the applicant needs some additional piece of cogent evidence inferring bias. Instead of solid evidence, however, the applicant has tendered allegations that, in admitting the complaint form, the adjudicator may have breached the
Privacy Act
, R.S.C. 1985, c. P-21,
or breached a rule concerning the admissibility of evidence. Neither of these allegations, however, bear up to scrutiny, as shown below. They do not amount, there­fore, to the solid evidence on which this court, weighing matters realistically, practically and thinking them through, would conclude that there was a reasonable ap­prehension of bias. This conclusion may also be applied to the other procedural decisions made by the adjudicator which the applicant alleges demonstrate a bias against him. Whether the admission of the complaint form by the adjudicator breaches the duty of fairness owed to the applicant will be examined below.
