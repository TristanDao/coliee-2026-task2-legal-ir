At page 208, Cattanach, J., adds this:
"The purpose of the statute is clear. The expropriated party is to be saved harmless from any cost caused him by the registration of a notice of intention to expropriate his property. He is entitled to ascertain his legal rights and the amount of compensation he may claim consequent upon the action of the Crown and he is to be reimbursed for the costs antecedent to the beginning of an action. He is entitled to his costs of the preparation of his case. That is what s. 27 provides.
"Likewise, the expropriated party is to be saved harmless from the costs of the action if the amount of the compensation awarded in an action exceeds that offered.
"Thus there are, in effect, two sets of costs to be taxed:
(1) those reasonably incurred by the expropriated party after the confirmation of the notice of intention to expropriate is registered up to the time proceedings are begun by filing a statement of claim - these may be referred to as s. 27 costs, and
(2) the costs of the proceedings begun by way of statement of claim and those incidental thereto on a solicitor-and-client basis if the award exceeds the offer - these may be referred to as s. 36 costs."
