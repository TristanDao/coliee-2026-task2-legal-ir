I have some hesitation in adopting this analogy. The application of these somewhat didactic categories of costs appears over the years to have created more confusion and obscurity than firm direction. In his updated edition of the Law of Costs (2nd Ed. Aurora: Canada Law Book, 1987), the author, Mark M. Orkin, at pages 1-2 deals at length with the classification of costs. Not only does the author deal with party and party costs, solicitor and client costs and costs as between a solicitor and his own client, but his gleaning of a multitude of cases dealing with different kinds of costs leads him to suggest that there might be not three but five classifications, adding, as a pithy comment, that in practice it is not always easy to distinguish between them.
