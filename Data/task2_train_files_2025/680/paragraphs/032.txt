In his lengthy reasons for judgment in Desjardins v. National Capital Commission (No. 2), supra, Cattanach, J., although dealing with costs per se and not with interest on some of these costs, says at page 207:
"By virtue of s. 27 the Crown shall pay to that person 'an amount equal to the legal, appraised and other costs reasonably incurred by him in asserting a claim for such compensation ...'.
"In the language of the section the word 'reasonably' modifies the word 'incurred'. Thus by that language it is the legal and appraisal costs that must be incurred with reason and the word in its context does not qualify the quantum of the legal and appraisal costs.
.....
"The costs payable to an expropriated party under s. 27(1), subject to the qualifications mentioned, are payable in any event. If a dispute should arise as to reasonable incurrence and unconscionable amount of those costs then s. 27(2) provides that the costs may be taxed.
"If the expropriated party is dissatisfied with the compensation offered and, as happened in this instance, the negotiation of a settlement of compensation payable, under s. 28 of the Act, fails then, as provided by s. 29, that party may commence proceedings in the Federal Court of Canada by statement of claim to ascertain the amount of compensation to which he is entitled.
"This the plaintiff did.
"Where the amount of the compensation adjudged to be payable exceeds the amount of the offer by the Crown then, by virtue of s. 36(2) the court shall direct that the whole of expropriated parties' costs of an incident to the proceedings (that is the proceedings begun by statement of claim) determined by the court on a solicitor-and-client basis, be paid by the Crown."
