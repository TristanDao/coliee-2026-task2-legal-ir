Further comments illustrative of these two statutory approaches to costs may be found in another judgment by Cattanach, J., in Camp Robin Hood Ltd. v. The Queen (1981), 21 L.C.R. 219, where he says at page 222:
"I accept the remarks made by my brother Walsh as to the purpose sought to be achieved by s. 36(2) of the Expropriation Act when he said in Trustee Board of Presbyterian Church in Canada v. The Queen (No. 2) (1976), 11 L.C.R. 305, at p. 306; [1977] 2 F.C. 107, at p. 108:
'The purpose of s. 36(2) would appear to be to indemnify the expropriated party against all costs which it will be called upon to pay "of the (sic) incident to the proceedings" so that the amount of the compensation will not be reduced by legal fees billed to it on a solicitor-and-client basis.'
"Bearing in mind that in the instances contemplated by s. 36(2) of the Act are not the cases where costs to be taxed on a solicitor-and-client basis are to be paid by the client (over which the client has a modicum of control) but rather cases where the costs are to be paid by the opposite party. Accordingly, the foregoing remarks cannot be construed as conferring a carte blanche upon the expropriated party but must be qualified by the well established elementary principles.
"One such principle is that many charges which are proper against the client are unjust and improper to fix upon the opposite party. When taxation is against the opposite party the taxation is on a less liberal scale than when the client is to pay. It is strictly a taxation of the costs of the action between party-and-party on a solicitor-and-client basis."
