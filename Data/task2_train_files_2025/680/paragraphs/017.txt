Perhaps, an analogy might be drawn in this respect with the historical categorization of the types of taxation to which different standards apply, namely party and party costs, solicitor-client costs and costs on a solicitor and his own client basis. It has been recognized in the case of Desjardins v. National Capital Commission (No. 2) (1982), 25 L.C.R. 205, at page 208, that costs pursuant to s. 36 of the Act are usually taxed on a solicitor-client basis and that costs under s. 27 of the statute deal with costs on a solicitor and his own client basis. It could then be argued that interest charges could be assessed under s. 27 but not, as the learned Prothonotary found it, assessable under s. 36.
