Ms. Thompson also referred to the Alternative Funding Agreement between the federal government and the Westbank Band pointing to several clauses which may provide some discretion in the allocation of funds received by the Band among objects for which the funds are provided so long as there be no fixing of funds for capital and noncapital purposes. The agreement appears so to provide but that is not relevant to the question whether a third party, not being an Indian, may levy by garnishing order a claim to funds paid by the federal government to the Band. She sought to distinguish cases cited by counsel for the band on the ground that at least one of them dealt with the circumstance where the garnishing order was obtained before judgment, a distinction from the case here that is not a circumstance which would permit the garnishing order to be effective if meaning is to be given to ss. 90(1) and 89(1) of the Indian Act. Finally, she pointed to the bank statement filed with affidavits of those affiants on behalf of the band and suggested that the statements might well be interpreted to indicate that the band operated more than one account from which transfers were made from the general account. That may be the case but the circumstances of this order were that it was directed to the general account of the Indian band.
