Counsel for Mr. Ates skilfully dismantled the decision of the Board and put it back together in such a way that at first blush it appears to be patently unreasonable. However, when looked at as a whole there is no patently unreasonable finding which would justify a judicial review. We must always keep in mind what Joyal, J., said in
Miranda v. Minister of Employment and Immigration
(1993), 63 F.T.R. 81 (T.D.):
"It is true that artful pleaders can find any number of errors when dealing with decisions of administrative tribunals."
The issue is whether an error is material to the decision reached. We have also been warned against over-zealous, microscopic examination of the file. See
Annalingam et al. v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
, [2000] F.T.R. Uned. 91; 6 Imm. L.R.(3d) 316 (T.D.);
Rivera-Velasquez v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
, [2003] F.T.R. Uned. 805; 2003 FC 1322, and
Agha v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
, [2004] F.T.R. Uned. 696; 2004 FC 1212.
