The Defendants argued that the Plaintiffs' action in negligence for breach of fiduciary duty must fail since they are owed no private law duty of care by the Defendants. The Prothonotary found, in the present instance, there to be an arguable private law duty owed by the Defendants to the Plaintiffs. The Prothonotary considered the principles of law summarized in
Fairford First Nation v. Canada (Attorney General)
, [1999] 2 F.C. 48; 156 F.T.R. 1 (T.D.), in respect to dependency or vulnerability required to establish a fiduciary obligation. He concluded that the principles in
Fairford
did not assist the Defendants in establishing that the Plaintiffs' case was plainly and obviously futile. The Prothonotary relied on
Devloo v. Canada
, [1991] F.C.J. No. 482; 129 N.R. 39 (F.C.A.), where the Federal Court of Appeal found that a private law duty may exist under the operative legislation; the legislation in that case was the
Canada Grain Act
, R.S.C. 1985, G-10. The Prothonotary determined in the present case, given the allegation of breach of statutory directive that it was arguable that the Defendants owed a private law duty to the Plaintiffs, and that an action for breach of fiduciary duty is not plainly and obviously futile.
