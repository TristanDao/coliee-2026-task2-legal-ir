The first notice of allegation concerned the '067 patent. The notice of allegation which caused this proceeding is referred to as the third notice of allegation, although it is only the second one concerning the '334 patent. This third notice of allegation was issued some months prior to the second prohibition order of MacKay, J. No one suggests that this notice of allegation is not "separate and distinct" from the first one concerning the '334 patent. Bayer has sug­gested, however, that the allegations in the second and third notices should have been combined in a single notice of allegation and that the failure by Apotex to do so consti­tutes abuse of process. A similar argument was rejected in
AB Hassle and Astra Pharma Inc. v. Canada (Min­ister of Na­tional Health and Welfare)
[see footnote 11] although the issue there was cast in terms of "res judicata in its broad applica­tion". I know of no authority which requires that separate and distinct allegations be made in the same notice although it may well be time efficient, particularly for "the second per­son", to proceed precisely in that manner. I am fully satisfied, on the basis of the Court of Appeal decision in
Eli Lilly & Co. v. Apotex Inc.
[see footnote 12] that there could have been no serious suggestion of abuse of process at the time this notice of allegation issued.
