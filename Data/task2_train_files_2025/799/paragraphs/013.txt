As I understand the applicant's argument, it is that in order to dismiss for undue delay, pursuant to rule 1617, there must not only have been unsatisfactorily explained delay but also a finding that the nondefaulting party has suffered or is likely to have suffered prejudice. Reference is made to the decisions in
Patex Snowmobiles Ltd. v. Bombardier Ltd. and Bombardier (Quebec) Ltd.
(1991), 48 F.T.R. 221 (T.D.);
Law Society of Manitoba v. Eadie
, [1988] 6 W.W.R. 354; 54 Man.R.(2d) 1 (C.A.), and
United Kingdom (Department of Transport) v. Smaller (Chris) (Transport) Ltd.
(1989), 103 N.R. 134 (H.L.). That is, it is argued that the requirements which must be met when an application to strike for want of prosecution is brought, for example, under rule 440, are applicable. In such case an applicant must demonstrate either that the delay was intentional and contumelious, or, that the delay was inordinate, inexcusable and prejudicial to the defendant (Although not relevant for present purposes, lengthy delay, in the context of rule 440, can itself raise a presumption of prejudice:
Canada v. Aqua-Gem Investments Ltd.
, [1993] 2 F.C. 425; 149 N.R. 273 (F.C.A.)).
