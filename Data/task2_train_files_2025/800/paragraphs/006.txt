However, Mrs. Huynh submits that the process is unfair. If the Applicant does not fare well on the writing test, she is called to appear in person before a Citizenship Judge. The forms do not specifically state that she is entitled to bring material which would at least give rise to the consideration of humanitarian issues. The imperfections of the forms were noted by Gibson J. in Maharatnam v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), [2000] F.C.J. 405. He said, and I agree, that since "most applicants appear before a Citizenship Judge without counsel, and are likely to be unfamiliar with the existence of humanitarian and compassionate discretion, in the interests of fairness, it might be useful to include a brief notice regarding the existence of discretion in the "NOTICE TO APPEAR"".
