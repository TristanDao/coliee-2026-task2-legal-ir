These cases discussed the burden of proof applicable when considering a 1F(a) exclusion. Instead of requiring a determination as to whether, on the balance of probabilities, there exist
serious reasons for considering
that a crime has been committed, the phrase "serious reasons for considering" has been interpreted as describing the burden of proof to be applied to a determination of
whether the individual has committed or been complicit in
one or more of the crimes. That burden of proof is lower than that required by a finding on the balance of probabilities.
