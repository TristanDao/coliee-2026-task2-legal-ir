In
Moreno
, the individual was a member of the Salvadorean army who had been forcibly recruited at the age of 16. He stood guard on one occasion during the torture of a prisoner, made no attempt to intervene because he thought it would be useless to do so, and deserted shortly thereafter. It was held that passive acquiescence to a crime against humanity was not enough to make a person an accomplice to that crime. In order to be complicit in a crime more than mere physical presence at the scene is necessary. In the
Moreno
case the court found there had been no prior knowledge by the applicant of the acts of torture that were to be committed and there had been no direct assistance or encouragement given by the applicant to his superiors in committing the crime.
