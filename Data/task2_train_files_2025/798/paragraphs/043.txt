Counsel for the applicant argues that the Board cannot both conclude that the group carried out attacks on civilian targets and observed the customs of war. Attacks on civilian targets are by definition breaches of the customs of war (although attacks on military or governmental targets that only incidentally involve the killing of civilians are not). Counsel also argues that insofar as crimes against humanity are concerned, it is not solely the period of time that the claimant was physically present in the camp that is relevant. As noted, one can be complicit in a crime without being physically present, for example, by providing funds to support the commission of the crime.
