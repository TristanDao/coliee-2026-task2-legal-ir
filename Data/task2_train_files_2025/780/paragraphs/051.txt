It is well-established that, pursuant to Rule 22 of the
Federal Court Immigration and Refugee Protection Rules
, SOR/2002-232, special reasons must exist for the Court to award costs on an application for judicial review. My review of the case law leads me to conclude that special reasons include situations where one party has acted in a manner that may be characterized as unfair, oppressive, improper or actuated by bad faith. But special reasons can also include conduct that unnecessarily or unreasonably prolongs the proceedings. See
Platonov v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
, [2000] F.C.J. No. 1438; 192 F.T.R. 260 (T.D.);
Singh (Bhupender) et al. v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
, [2005] F.T.R. Uned. 350; 2005 FC 544; [2005] F.C.J. No. 669;
John Doe v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) et al.
, [2006] F.T.R. Uned. 318; 2006 FC 535; and
Johnson v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
(2005), 275 F.T.R. 316; 2005 FC 1262, at paragraph 26.
