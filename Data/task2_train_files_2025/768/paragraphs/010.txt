Turning to the first of those factors, decisions of the Board are not protected by a strong privative clause (see Pushpanathan v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), [1998] 1 S.C.R. 982) [see footnote 1], thus this factor does not favour great deference. However, viewing the legislative scheme as a whole, and the provisions pertaining to refugee claim determinations - the third factor cited above - the Board is given a wide degree of discretion and authority, supporting a higher measure of judicial respect: see for e.g., sections 96, 97, 161-166, and 170 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act, supra in particular.
