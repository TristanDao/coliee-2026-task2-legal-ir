The question of whether an officer applied the correct test in assessing risk in an humanitarian and compassionate application is a question of law, and it has been held to be reviewable on the standard of correctness. See, for example,
Pinter v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration)
, [2005] F.T.R. Uned. 188; [2005] F.C.J. No. 366 (F.C.), at paragraphs 3-5. Legal questions of central importance to the legal system as a whole and outside a decision-maker's specialized area of expertise continue to attract scrutiny on a correctness standard. Questions of law that do not rise to this level may be compatible with a reasonableness standard. See:
Dunsmuir
at paragraphs 55 and 60. Having regard to the absence of a privative clause, the relative lack of expertise on the part of an officer to appreciate whether he or she has applied the wrong test at law, and the importance of ensuring that officers apply the test that Parliament has prescribed, I conclude that the question of whether the officer applied the correct test is reviewable on the correctness standard.
