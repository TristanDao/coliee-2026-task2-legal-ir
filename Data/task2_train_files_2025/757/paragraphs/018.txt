I turn now to the cases referred to in
Bhatti
.
Surujpal
involved two applicants, a husband and wife. The evidence pertained primarily to the husband. The wife's grandfather was murdered by the police in an apparent search for the husband. The term "indirect persecution" is not found in the decision. While the decision is not explicit on the point, it seems as if the evidence of persecution of the husband and killing of the grandfather indicated that the wife would be in danger herself. When it is remembered that the concept of a well-founded fear of persecution is forward-looking, and that past persecution is relevant to indicate what could transpire in the future, it seems that
Surujpal
simply stands for the proposition that evidence of past persecution of other family members may be a basis for an applicant's own well-founded fear of persecution in the future.
