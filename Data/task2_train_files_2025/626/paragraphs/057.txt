The fourth element of the
Nelles
test, that the defendant proceeded maliciously or without the primary purpose of bringing the law into effect, was described thus:
"The required element of malice is, for all intents, the equivalent of 'improper purpose'. It has according to Flemming, a 'wider meaning than spite, ill-will or a spirit of vengeance, and includes any other improper purpose, such as to gain a private collateral advantage ...'"
In
Roncarelli v. Duplessis
[see footnote 11] Rand, J., characterized malice by a public body as "... simply acting for a reason and purpose knowingly foreign to the administration ...". The plaintiffs have a difficult task to establish the element of malice as the reason and purpose of the action by public officials.
