Mr. Krantz then addressed Mr. Mahon's affidavit and specifically the five studies appended to Mr. Mahon's affidavit. For various reasons set out in his affidavit, Mr. Krantz concluded the studies referred to by Mr. Mahon were not relevant. For example, he said the 1994 study of the DSI test strips were not relevant because the quality control standards used then were lower than the tighter acceptance standards used today and this study was done with the original ONE TOUCH metre which was removed from the Canadian market which LifeScan stopped manufacturing as it was deemed not as accurate as the current models. He indicates LifeScan submitted this study in the LifeScan U.S. proceeding as evidence the strips did not perform equally. He noted the U.S. federal judge who presided over the lawsuit ruled against LifeScan's claim stating "... in fact, the evidence presented to the Court to date shows the defendant's (DSI's) product is comparable to the plaintiff's (LifeScan's) product ...".
