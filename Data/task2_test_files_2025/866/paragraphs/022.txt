This is not an easy task. If forcing the applicant to discovery on issues relating to its profits would facilitate the possibility of settlement, as is alleged by the plaintiff, and if settlement resulted then there would be no doubt that denial of an order under rule 480 would work to minimize the expense of the action. But the applicant denies that early discovery on issues relating to its profits would facilitate settlement. The applicant alleges that the plaintiffs have sufficient information regarding its sales and costs related to sales of the product in question to adequately articulate an offer of settlement should they wish to do so. In support of its position, the applicant points to the fact that Paul Herbert, when examined for discovery on his affidavit on behalf of the plaintiffs on this motion, refused to answer questions on the issue of the plaintiffs' knowledge of the sales and costs of the applicant. It was argued before me that, based on
Levesque et al. v. Comeau et al.
(1970), 5 N.B.R.(2d) 15; 16 D.L.R.(3d) 425 (S.C.C.), I am entitled to draw an adverse inference against the plaintiffs, as to their knowledge, based on this refusal to answer and should in fact, do so.
