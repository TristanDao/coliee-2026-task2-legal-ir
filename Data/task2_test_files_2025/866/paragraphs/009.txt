In
Procter & Gamble Co. et al. v. Kimberly - Clark of Canada Ltd.
, (1988) 21 C.P.R.(3d) 414 (F.C.T.D.), affirmed on appeal (1988), 23 C.P.R.(3d) 287 (F.C.A.), Joyal, J., stated:
"This kind of case-splitting finds favour in many patent infringement proceedings. No doubt its process is usually attractive to both parties. It facilitates and expedites the trial of the infringement issue leaving to a subsequent reference, if such should be found necessary, the determination of damages.
"The affidavit filed by the plaintiffs in support of this motion appears at first blush to be quite persuasive. It is certain that for purposes of the trial and of all proceedings leading to it, the infringement issues raised in the pleadings are sufficiently complex that leaving the damages issue to a later date would certainly expedite the whole process and avoid long and laborious enquiries into damages which might later on prove to be pointless. In a normal case, therefore, it would be logical and reasonable to proceed in that fashion.
And later:
"It would also appear that rule 480 has not often been applied without the consent of the parties. In
Brouwer Turf Equipment Ltd. v. A. and M. Sod Supply Ltd.
, [1977] 1 F.C. 51; 13 N.R. 83, the Federal Court of Appeal stated that as rule 480 is an exceptional procedure, a reference should not be ordered absent consent of the parties or reasons bearing on the conduct of the action as a whole."
