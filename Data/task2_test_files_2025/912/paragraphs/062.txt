It was in
R. v. Morgentaler
, [1988] 1 S.C.R. 30; 82 N.R. 1; 26 O.A.C. 1, that the Supreme Court of Canada first associated the defect of vagueness with the principles of fundamental justice. (Patrice Garant,
Droit administratif
, 3rd Ed., Les Éditions Yvon Blais Inc., Cowansville, 1991, vol. III, at pp. 416-417.) In that case, Dickson, C.J., held that the impugned legislative provision violated the principles of fundamental justice because of its "failure to provide an adequate standard for therapeutic abortion committees". (
R. v. Morgentaler
, at pp. 68-69. A majority of judges concurred with him on this point.) Subsequently, in the
Reference Re Sections 193 and 195.1(1)(c) of the Criminal Code
, [1990] 1 S.C.R. 1123; 109 N.R. 81; 68 Man.R.(2d) 1, Lamer, J. (as he then was), with whom a majority of judges concurred, expressed his views in terms similar to those used by Dickson, C.J., in
Morgentaler
.
