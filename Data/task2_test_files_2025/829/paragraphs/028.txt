The respondent argues that the Minister's discretion is not so limited: the statute says the Minister "may" issue a permit. It is argued that this gives very broad discretion to the Minister to take into account a variety of public policy factors. It is argued that the Minister is not limited to adequate supply and distribution purposes but may also consider broad general policy concerns. Alternatively, it is argued that he may at least take into account considerations relevant to the subsections of s. 3 other than s. 3(e). That is, it is argued that the Minister did not take into account extraneous factors because he is entitled to consider not only s. 3(e) of the
Export and Import Control Act
but also ss. 3(b), 3(c.1) and 3(d). Alternatively if only s. 3(e) is relevant, then that section should be broadly construed so that "other needs" encompasses the purposes of ensuring processing within one province or federal provincial commitments with that province or international trade considerations.
