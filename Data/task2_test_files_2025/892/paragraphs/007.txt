For the last quoted statement, Veit, J., cites
Clarke v. Law
(1855), 2 K. & J. 28; 69 E.R. 680. In a subsequent case also cited and quoted by Veit, J.,
Re Quartz Hill
(1882), 21 Ch.D. 642, Jessel, M.R., stated by reference to
Clarke v. Law
:
"Every rule of procedure ought to be framed with a view to its being such that if it be properly acted upon the course of justice will be kept pure and undefiled. With this view I think it most important that no person who has taken a step with a view to gaining an advantage should be at liberty to withdraw it if he finds that it will not answer. Lord Hatherley accordingly held that where a party had proposed himself at a witness and filed an affidavit, he could not, when threatened with cross-examination, withdraw the affidavit and escape cross-examination. I cannot imagine anything more calculated to bring the course of justice into contempt than to allow a person to file evidence which if there is no cross-examination makes in his favour, but which he knows will break down on cross-examination, and then to withdraw it if he finds that cross-examination is threatened."
